# What this is, and what it is not

This catalogue records the **visual morphology** of every plant drawing in the herbal and pharmaceutical sections of the manuscript: growth habit, leaf form, flower, and root, plus a judgement of botanical realism and — only where a non-specialist would clearly recognise it — a **speculative, visual-only** candidate genus. Six independent analysts each examined a block of pages.

It is essential to be clear about the limits:

- **This is not decoding.** The Voynichese text beside each plant is undeciphered; there is no caption to confirm any identification. Nothing here reads the script.
- **Identification is mostly impossible by design.** The plants are overwhelmingly **stylised or chimeric** — a plausible leaf grafted onto an invented flower and, very often, a botanically impossible root. Such composites do not correspond to any single real species.
- **Every candidate is a hypothesis, not a fact.** Each is tagged `[SPECULATIVE — visual only]`. Per a strict no-fabrication standard, the candidate column was left blank wherever there was no clear resemblance — which is the large majority of pages. Latin names were never invented to fill space.

## Aggregate result (≈136 plant folios)

| Realism class | Count (approx.) | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| REALISTIC | ~4 | Parts form a botanically coherent real plant (e.g. p-061, p-093, p-107, p-118) |
| STYLISED-but-plausible | ~100 | Recognisable plant architecture, but no secure species |
| COMPOSITE-CHIMERA | ~29 | Impossible combination (e.g. octopus/animal-shaped or segmented roots) |
| UNIDENTIFIABLE | ~3 | Too faded or abstract to assess (p-039, p-095, p-098) |

**Only ~9 of ~136 folios (~7%) showed even a tentative, lay-recognisable resemblance to a real genus**, and not one is confident. The candidates flagged were: Viola (p-020), an Asteraceae-type daisy (p-029), Centaurea (p-033), Geranium/Aconitum (p-045), Daucus/carrot (p-082, p-117, p-165), Myosotis (p-094), and Aquilegia (p-113) — all `[SPECULATIVE — visual only]`.

A recurring and telling feature: **the roots are the least realistic element.** Many are frankly fantastical — splayed "octopus" arms (p-110, p-159, p-180, p-182), segmented caterpillar-like rhizomes (p-036, p-044), looped/knotted forms (p-103), or roots drawn as **animals** with legs and tails (p-166). Small creatures are also tucked into roots and crowns on p-050, p-054, p-055 (octopus-like), p-058, and p-068. This dominance of impossible underground organs is itself strong evidence that the herbal is not a faithful botanical record but a stylised or invented one — exactly why a century of identification attempts (O'Neill's "sunflower," Tucker's "Mexican plants") has failed.

# Bottom line

Across roughly 136 plant folios, the honest yield is: about four genuinely realistic drawings, a large majority of recognisable-but-unplaceable stylised plants, and around thirty outright botanical impossibilities — concentrated, tellingly, in the roots. Fewer than one in ten folios offers even a tentative visual resemblance to a real genus, and none is secure. This pattern is not a failure of effort; it is a property of the manuscript. The herbal was not drawn as an accurate botanical record, which is why neither this analysis nor any prior one can deliver a verified species list — and why no honest analyst should claim to.
